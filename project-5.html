My Time at Nokia

Nokia · IxD Trainee · 2025–2026 · Enterprise B2B

Internal Tooling & Customer-Facing Products

The Setup

I joined Nokia as an IxD Trainee within the Cloud and Network Services division. Over one year, I worked primarily on NgDS — an internal platform for network dimensioning and configuration — and towards the end, contributed to Autonomous Networks, a customer-facing suite for network automation.

Timeline: 1 year · Tools: Figma, Nokia Design Systems (Freeform & Connect), Cursor · Team: Up to 3 designers per product.

Experience

Learning to Work in a Real Product Team

The first thing Nokia taught me had nothing to do with design tools. It was how to work well with other people, how to collaborate, how to communicate, when to push and when to listen.

Working in a real product environment means you're never designing in isolation. There are engineers, product managers, architects, stakeholders, all with different priorities and different constraints. Learning to navigate that, to be someone the team could rely on, was its own kind of growth.

I also learned that in product design, you don't need to understand everything before you start. You need to understand enough to execute your task well, and then keep learning as you go. Getting lost in the details too early is its own kind of trap.

Designing Within Constraints

NgDS (Next Generation Dimensioning and Configuration Solution) was built to replace the fragmented spreadsheets Nokia engineers use to calculate and configure network resources. One platform, centralizing what was previously scattered across disconnected tools.

The users are domain specialists. The product is data-heavy. And many of the structural and technical decisions had already been made before I arrived, decisions that couldn't be revisited.

A lot of the work was about finding the best possible solution within a fixed set of boundaries. That required a different mindset than starting from scratch: less about invention, more about precision. Where is there room to improve? Where isn't there? Learning to read that difference, quickly was one of the most practical skills I developed.

But working within constraints doesn't mean accepting everything. I also learned that a product designer's job isn't just to execute what's asked. It's to understand the technical landscape well enough to have a real conversation, to push back when something doesn't serve the product vision, to propose alternatives, to bring a critical perspective to the table alongside the architects and engineers.

Seeing the Gap: QA and Usability Testing

Two moments in the process made the gap between intent and reality very concrete.

The first was QA. After the MVP beta, we built a structured review system to compare implemented screens against the design, categorizing issues by severity across spacing, typography, states, interactions, and missing elements. What you design and what gets built are not always the same thing. Seeing that difference clearly, and communicating it precisely, was a skill I hadn't expected to develop on this project.

The second was usability testing. I helped define the design goals and KPIs for the beta testing phase, and then watched real users interact with something I had helped build. The moments where they got stuck, confused, or took an unexpected path, those were more informative than any design review. It changed how I approach assumptions.

From One Product to Five: The Welcome Kit

Early in the project, my lead asked me to build an onboarding document for NgDS. She hadn't had time to onboard me properly, and the idea was practical: it would help me get to know the product deeply, and leave something useful for the next person who joined.

What started as a structured way to learn became a resource that outlasted the immediate need. The document covered what NgDS is, how the team works, key concepts, and where to find everything, the kind of context that usually lives in people's heads and disappears when teams change.

When three other products within the same platform pipeline saw what had been created, they decided to adopt the same approach. We ran a series of working sessions, one representative per product, myself moderating and setting the agenda, to define a shared structure that would work across all five products. From those sessions, I built a common template that each team then filled in with their own product's information.

The task was given to me. The outcome grew beyond it. and what I learned was that reliability is a design contribution. Keeping files organized, delivering on time, documenting decisions clearly — these aren't peripheral to design work. In a team under pressure, they're part of what makes good work possible.

A Product Is Never Finished

One of the bigger lessons from NgDS was about pace and scope. A product is something you keep working on, it's never done. And one of the best things you can do, especially early on, is start in parts. Breaking the work into smaller pieces keeps the team moving, keeps the thinking clear, and prevents the kind of overwhelm that comes from trying to solve everything at once.

The timeline shifted more than once. Requirements arrived late. Priorities changed. Learning to stay steady through that — to keep delivering quality without waiting for perfect conditions — was something I had to build over time.

Projects

Switching Contexts: Autonomous Networks

Towards the end of the internship, I was pulled in to support a separate team working on Autonomous Networks — Nokia's customer-facing suite for network automation, built on Nokia's Connect design system. Unlike Freeform, which I had been working with throughout NgDS, Connect is Nokia's newer design language — more modern in approach, with a visual direction that felt noticeably different from what I was used to.

AN is a customer-facing product — designed for telecom operators, C-level decision makers, and network administrators, each with different goals and different levels of technical depth. The product needed to communicate complex network data clearly and compellingly across all of them. I worked on chart and dashboard design, explored side navigation concepts, and contributed to filtering toolbar patterns.

The nature of the work was different from NgDS — more visual, more open-ended, with more room to explore and propose. Working on a product built around the needs of such varied external users, where clarity and visual quality directly affect how people understand their networks, gave me a clearer sense of the kind of design work I want to do more of.

Contributions

What I Built Along the Way

Beyond the screen design work, some contributions shaped how the team operated:

QA framework — a severity-based review system (Low / Medium / High) to communicate implementation issues clearly to developers across spacing, typography, states, interactions, and missing elements.

Component library audit — reviewed NgDS components against the NCOM library to identify what could be shared, what needed to be built, and what risked becoming inconsistent over time.

Dark mode compliance — reviewed every screen to ensure color, contrast, and hierarchy held up in dark mode.
